How to Inject a Market-Regime Signal into a Reinforcement-Learning Portfolio Policy}

\begin{abstract}
Reinforcement-learning (RL) agents for portfolio management are typically
trained against a single stationary reward landscape, yet markets alternate
between structurally distinct regimes in which the optimal allocation policy
differs. Given an \emph{external} regime signal --- here, the posterior of a
4-state Gaussian Hidden Markov Model (HMM) fitted on macroeconomic ETFs,
disjoint from the trading universe --- we ask the architectural question:
how should the signal enter the policy network? We compare (i) \emph{hard
switching}, which discretely selects one of four regime-specialist heads;
(ii) \emph{soft gating}, which blends all four heads by the HMM posterior;
and (iii) a \emph{learned router} trained end-to-end without consuming the
posterior directly, against a regime-agnostic baseline, on 38 U.S. equities
(2015--2024). We evaluate under two on-policy algorithms (A2C and PPO), with
seed-paired statistics, an expanding 8-window walk-forward protocol,
Jobson--Korkie--Memmel tests on daily returns, and controls that match
training mechanics, regularisation, and hyperparameters across arms. The
headline is an audit result: \textbf{once these are controlled, no
architectural difference survives}. A no-gate baseline matches both gated
variants on the single-period protocol (Sharpe 0.40 vs.\ 0.42/0.46,
within seed noise) and on the walk-forward (1.27 vs.\ 1.27/1.11), where
frequent retraining substitutes for regime awareness. An apparent
soft-over-hard dominance (0.46 vs.\ 0.14) dissolved when a regularisation
asymmetry was matched (0.46 vs.\ 0.42); hard switching's seed instability
was likewise regularisation-driven, not architectural. What does survive
every pass: the learned router is the worst and least stable variant
under both algorithms --- a stable, separately estimated regime signal
beats jointly learned routing --- and a hand-crafted HMM-gated
equal-weight rule \emph{destroys} value (Sharpe 0.41 $\rightarrow$ 0.16),
so the signal is not directly monetizable either. Along the way we
dissolve an internally consistent, statistically significant pro-gating
narrative manufactured by a sliding-window update scheme (a drawdown
advantage at $p{=}0.0025$, a walk-forward ranking, a COVID stress-test
story), and we identify measurable mechanisms behind the null:
near-one-hot posteriors that make soft and hard gating coincide on
${\sim}80\%$ of days, a $20\times$ gradient-occupancy imbalance that
starves rare-regime experts, regime features recoverable from the
agent's own state, and an always-fully-invested single-asset-class
action space that cannot express regime rotation. Architectural
comparisons in RL finance can be artifacts of training mechanics and
asymmetric tuning; we propose mechanics-and-regularisation ablations,
plus a no-gate control, as a standard robustness pass.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
%% ===================================================================

Deep reinforcement learning has become a standard alternative to classical
mean--variance optimisation for portfolio allocation, learning sequential,
state-dependent policies directly from data~\cite{jiang2017drl,liu2020finrl}.
A persistent weakness is non-stationarity: markets transition between
qualitatively different regimes --- sustained bulls, sharp crashes, choppy
recoveries, prolonged bears --- each of which may demand a different
allocation policy. A single network trained to perform well \emph{on
average} risks being mediocre in every regime rather than excellent in any.

This motivates \emph{regime-aware RL} (RA-RL), in which the agent receives
an explicit signal about the current regime. Prior work has largely focused
on whether such signals help at all. We address the question one level
down, which prior work leaves open: \textbf{given a regime signal, how
should it be incorporated into the policy network?} We compare three
mechanisms within one framework, holding data, reward, universe, and
training budget fixed:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Hard RA-RL} --- the maximum-a-posteriori regime label
        discretely selects one of $K$ regime-specialist policy heads;
  \item \textbf{Soft RA-RL} --- the full $K$-dimensional HMM posterior
        blends all heads' outputs as a probability-weighted mixture;
  \item \textbf{Learned Router} --- a gating MLP trained end-to-end
        produces its own routing weights, isolating the value of the
        \emph{external} signal from the soft-blending mechanism itself.
\end{enumerate}
All three are benchmarked against a regime-agnostic baseline, classical
allocators (equal-weight, Markowitz, buy-and-hold), and --- critically ---
a \emph{non-RL} rule that consumes the same HMM signal, which tests whether
RL is needed at all.

The regime signal is a 4-state Gaussian HMM~\cite{hamilton1989} fitted,
unsupervised and offline, on six macroeconomic ETFs disjoint from the
trading universe. This separates regime \emph{detection} (stable, external,
estimated once) from regime \emph{exploitation} (the policy's use of the
signal) --- a separation whose value the learned-router ablation directly
quantifies.

Because RL results in finance are notoriously fragile, we subject every
claim to four robustness passes that we believe should be standard:
(a)~\textbf{algorithm robustness} --- every comparison is run under both
A2C~\cite{mnih2016a2c} and PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, which differ exactly
in trust-region control; (b)~\textbf{seed-paired statistics} --- all
variants share seeds, so we use paired $t$ and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests,
plus Jobson--Korkie--Memmel~\cite{jobson1981,memmel2003} tests on daily
returns; (c)~\textbf{mechanics robustness} --- we re-ran the full
comparison after replacing a stride-1 sliding-window update buffer with
non-overlapping batches; and (d)~\textbf{tuning symmetry} --- we re-ran
the comparison after matching a regularisation coefficient that had been
tuned per-arm during development, and re-ran the walk-forward soft arm at
its tuned hyperparameters. Successive passes dissolved successive claims.
Soft gating's maximum-drawdown advantage --- the most statistically
significant result under the original mechanics ($p{=}0.0025$, paired) ---
reversed sign under corrected batching; its risk-adjusted-return
advantage survived the mechanics fix but collapsed to a tie under matched
regularisation; and a no-gate control landed inside the same band as both
gated variants on both protocols. We report this trajectory openly as the
paper's central contribution: architectural conclusions in RL-for-finance
can be manufactured by the update scheme and by asymmetric tuning, and
the controls that catch this belong in the evaluation protocol.

\textbf{Contributions.}
(1)~The first controlled comparison, within one RL portfolio framework, of
an externally supplied regime gate, a discretised version of the same
gate, a learned end-to-end router, and a no-gate control --- under matched
mechanics, regularisation, and seeds. The result is a robust null: no
gating mechanism measurably beats no gate, on either evaluation protocol.
(2)~The one architecture-level finding that survives every pass: the
learned router is the worst and least stable variant under both A2C and
PPO --- external, separately estimated regime signals beat jointly
learned routing.
(3)~A mechanism analysis of the null with measured quantities: the HMM
posterior is one-hot ($>0.99$) on ${\sim}80\%$ of days, rare-regime
expert heads receive $20\times$ less gradient mass, the gate's features
are recoverable from the agent's own state, and the fully-invested
equity-only action space cannot express the regime rotation the signal
is informative about.
(4)~A reproducibility case study: six publishable-looking claims ---
including a spurious $p{=}0.0025$ drawdown advantage and a COVID
stress-test story --- dissolved one by one under mechanics correction,
regularisation matching, hyperparameter parity, and a no-gate control,
with the full audit trail reported.

\section{Related Work}
%% ===================================================================

\textbf{RL for portfolio management.}
Policy-gradient and actor-critic methods have been applied to portfolio
allocation~\cite{jiang2017drl,moody2001,mnih2016a2c}, often via
frameworks such as FinRL~\cite{liu2020finrl}, with recent work refining
the reward side of the problem~\cite{karzanov2025regret}; these
typically train a single policy without explicit regime conditioning.
Sun et al.~\cite{sun2022deeptrader} route stock predictions through
uncertainty-aware expert sub-models, but their routing addresses
\emph{model} uncertainty over predictors, not market-regime conditioning
of an RL policy.

\textbf{Regime-switching models.}
Hamilton's Markov-switching framework~\cite{hamilton1989} established that
returns exhibit persistent discrete regimes well captured by latent-state
models; HMM-derived probabilities have since been used for allocation
mostly within classical optimisation, including point-in-time macro-regime
models for factor investing~\cite{nuriyev2024macro} and regime-aware
sample weighting for forecasting~\cite{jang2025regime}. HMM generative
models have also been used to simulate equity dynamics for downstream
decision systems~\cite{istiaque2023hmm}. Closest to us, Nixon
Raj~\cite{nixonraj2025} benchmarks PPO variants under \emph{latent} regime
shifts inferred implicitly, and Verma et al.~\cite{verma2026} pair a
Markov-switching model with RL for three-asset tactical allocation.
Neither isolates our architectural question: given an \emph{explicit},
externally estimated regime posterior, what is the best way to inject it
into the policy? We hold the universe to a single asset class precisely to
keep this comparison clean.

\textbf{Mixture-of-experts and gating.}
MoE~\cite{jacobs1991} and its large-scale
descendants~\cite{shazeer2017moe} train the gate jointly with the experts
on the same objective. Soft RA-RL borrows the specialists-plus-blend
structure but deliberately departs from MoE: the blend weights are the
posterior of an HMM fitted offline on disjoint data and receive no
gradient from the portfolio reward. MoEDRLPM~\cite{moedrlpm2025} is a
contemporaneous RL portfolio model with an expert pool and a
\emph{learned} router in the classical MoE sense --- architecturally our
Learned Router arm, whose instability we document under two algorithms;
learned MoE gating has likewise been applied to trade
execution~\cite{xu2025moe}.

\textbf{Latent context inference in RL.}
An alternative to an external signal is to infer context from interaction,
as in VariBAD~\cite{zintgraf2020varibad}. Our Learned Router is
conceptually in this family, and its seed collapses under both A2C and PPO
are consistent with the known difficulty of learning context inference
jointly with control.

\textbf{The 1/N puzzle.}
DeMiguel et al.~\cite{demiguel2009} showed naive equal weighting often
beats optimised portfolios out of sample. We replicate this in the
walk-forward protocol, scope our claims accordingly, and add a twist: on
the 2022--2024 single-period test, the soft-gated agent beats 1/N.

\section{Methodology}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{Problem Formulation}
Portfolio allocation is a continuous-control MDP. Each trading day $t$ the
agent observes state $s_t$ and outputs target weights $w_t$ over $N{=}38$
assets with $\sum_i w_{t,i}=1$ and $w_{t,i}\in[-0.05,0.20]$ (limited
shorting, 20\% position cap). Raw policy outputs are mapped to this
bounded simplex by Euclidean projection, computed by bisection on the
projection's Lagrange multiplier.

\subsection{Regime Detection}
A $K{=}4$-state Gaussian HMM is fitted on daily features of six
macroeconomic ETFs (SPY, DBC, LQD, EMB, TLT, TIP --- equities,
commodities, credit, EM debt, duration, inflation-protected), using
training-period data only (no lookahead). States are ordered by a
momentum-minus-volatility score into an interpretable bear-to-bull
ordering. The HMM emits either a hard MAP label or the full posterior
$p_t\in\Delta^3$, depending on the consuming architecture.
$K{=}4$ is selected by BIC: it beats $K{=}5$ (11948 vs.\ 11998), and
larger $K$ wins only via degenerate states with 0.3--2\% occupancy
(Figure~\ref{fig:bic}). \pending{K=2/3 end-to-end ablation if budget
allows; currently queued}

\subsection{Architectural Variants}
All variants share a 1{,}596-dimensional base state: the flattened
$38{\times}38$ return covariance (1{,}444) plus MACD/RSI/CCI/ADX per asset
(152). They differ only in what regime information is appended and how the
actor uses it. All actors share a two-layer 256-unit ReLU MLP trunk and a
state-independent log-std Gaussian head.

\textbf{Baseline (no gate).} Single Gaussian head; the regime slot in
the state is replaced by a constant, so state dimensionality is
identical and the arm is a pure information-removal control.

\textbf{Hard RA-RL.} The MAP label (scalar) is appended; four specialist
heads share the trunk; the label masks all but one head per step, so only
one head receives gradient per update.

\textbf{Soft RA-RL.} The 4-dim posterior is appended; the same four heads
are all evaluated every step and their outputs combined as
$a_t=\sum_k p_t(k)\, a_t^{(k)}$ --- the posterior \emph{is} the gate; no
gating parameters exist.

\textbf{Learned Router.} Identical experts-and-blend structure, but a
router MLP consuming the trunk features and the raw posterior produces the
blend weights, trained end-to-end with the policy. This isolates the value
of using the external signal \emph{directly}.

\subsection{Reward}
All variants optimise the same mean--variance-penalised reward
\[
r_t = \big(\Delta_t - \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda\, w_t^{\top}\Sigma_t w_t
      - c\cdot \mathrm{TO}_t - \kappa \textstyle\sum_i w_{t,i}^2\big)\times s,
\]
with realised return $\Delta_t$, covariance $\Sigma_t$, turnover penalty
$c{=}10^{-4}$, concentration penalty $\kappa{=}0.005$, risk aversion
$\lambda{=}0.5$, and fixed scaling $s$. Identical rewards ensure
performance differences are attributable to architecture.

\subsection{Training Algorithms and the Mechanics Ablation}
\label{sec:mechanics}
Each architecture is trained under \textbf{two} on-policy algorithms:
A2C~\cite{mnih2016a2c} and PPO~\cite{schulman2017ppo}, which differ
exactly in trust-region control (PPO's clipped surrogate bounds per-update
policy movement; A2C has no such bound). Both use GAE
($\lambda{=}0.95$)~\cite{schulman2016gae} with advantage normalisation,
entropy bonus, linear LR annealing ($1.0\times\rightarrow0.1\times$), and
Adam. Updates are computed on \emph{non-overlapping} batches of 20
transitions. Training runs 1000 epochs; evaluation is greedy (distribution
mean).

The non-overlapping-batch choice is itself a finding. An earlier version
of the pipeline used a stride-1 sliding window, producing an order of
magnitude more highly correlated overlapping updates per episode. Under
A2C this induced an early peak followed by sustained degradation
\emph{specific to Hard RA-RL} --- and, more insidiously, manufactured a
large, highly significant maximum-drawdown advantage for Soft RA-RL
($p{=}0.0025$ paired, $n{=}9$) that \emph{reversed sign} once the batching
was corrected, under both algorithms
(Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanics}). All results in this paper use the
corrected mechanics unless explicitly marked otherwise.

One development asymmetry required its own control: the L2
output-penalty coefficient had been tuned per architecture (0.5 for
Hard, 0.01 for Soft/Router). Because this penalty shrinks raw policy
outputs toward zero --- which the simplex projection maps toward equal
weights --- it is a de-facto regulariser toward $1/N$ and plausibly
consequential. Section~\ref{sec:results-single} reports the matched
control (Hard re-run at 0.01, shared seeds): the asymmetry, not the
architecture, accounted for both the apparent soft dominance and hard
switching's seed instability. All matched-arm comparisons in this paper
use L2${=}0.01$ unless marked otherwise.

\section{Experimental Setup}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{Data and Universe}
38 large-cap U.S. equities (Appendix~\ref{app:universe}) with complete
daily history 2015-01-01 to 2024-01-01, from Yahoo Finance via
FinRL~\cite{liu2020finrl}; the fixed universe implies mild survivorship
bias, disclosed not corrected. The HMM basket is disjoint from the
trading universe.

\subsection{Protocols}
\textbf{Single-period:} train 2015--2021, test 2022-01-01 to 2024-01-01
(a bear year plus a recovery year); 3 seeds per (architecture, algorithm).
\textbf{Walk-forward:} 8 expanding-train windows, six-month non-overlapping
test periods 2020--2023 (COVID crash, recovery, 2022 bear, 2023 bull);
2 seeds per window; per-window HMM refit (no lookahead).

\subsection{Baselines}
Equal-weight (1/N), Markowitz mean--variance, S\&P~500 buy-and-hold, an
LSTM-context RL baseline (temporal context without an explicit regime
signal), and two \emph{HMM-gated rule} baselines consuming the same
signal as the RL agents: bear$\rightarrow$cash (hard rule) and equity
exposure $=1-P(\mathrm{Bear})$ (soft rule), otherwise 1/N.

\subsection{Statistics}
All architecture comparisons share seeds, so we report one-tailed
\emph{paired} $t$ and Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (pairing removes
between-seed variance; the unpaired Welch tests used in early versions of
this work are uniformly weaker). Walk-forward comparisons use Wilcoxon
over the 8 paired per-window Sharpes and Jobson--Korkie--Memmel
tests~\cite{jobson1981,memmel2003} on the pooled ${\sim}1000$-day daily
return series. \pending{Holm--Bonferroni across the metric family for the
headline table}

\subsection{Single-Period: the Gap Dissolves under Matched
Regularisation, and No Gate Matches Both Gates}
\label{sec:results-single}

Table~\ref{tab:single-a2c} tells the single-period story in three
columns. At the \emph{development} configuration (Hard at L2${=}0.5$,
Soft at 0.01), soft gating appears dominant: Sharpe 0.462 vs.\ 0.142,
return $+26.2\%$ vs.\ $+0.3\%$, every soft seed positive while two of
three hard seeds collapse.\footnote{Under the \emph{original} update
mechanics this comparison was significant on all four metrics at pooled
$n{=}9$ --- Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanics} shows why we do not rest
claims on those runs.} This is the kind of result that gets published.
It does not survive its own control experiment.

\textbf{Matching the regularisation closes the gap.} Re-running Hard at
Soft's L2${=}0.01$, same seeds, moves it to Sharpe $0.422\pm0.282$ ---
a statistical dead heat with Soft on every metric (paired Sharpe
$p_1{=}0.44$; seed-pairs split 2--1). The seed instability also
vanishes: all three Hard seeds are positive at 0.01 (minimum $+0.126$),
so ``discrete switching is unstable under A2C'' was in fact ``L2${=}0.5$
is a bad setting.'' Notably, \emph{which} seed performs best flips with
the coefficient (Hard's only good seed at 0.5 becomes its worst at
0.01) --- the seed-level landscape is regularisation-dependent, another
warning against reading architecture from few-seed comparisons.

\textbf{The no-gate control lands in the same band.} The information-
removal baseline scores Sharpe $0.402$ (seeds: 0.256, 0.548; third seed
pending compute and marked in Appendix~\ref{app:perseed}). Paired deltas
on shared seeds are $+0.006$ and $+0.187$ (Soft$-$Baseline) and $+0.432$,
$-0.096$ (Hard$-$Baseline) --- small relative to the ${\sim}0.25$ seed
noise, with unstable sign. \textbf{On the protocol where a regime gate
should pay most --- train once, generalise across an unseen
bear-plus-recovery --- removing the regime signal entirely does not
measurably hurt.}

\subsection{Learned Router: Collapse under Both Algorithms}
\label{sec:results-router}

Under A2C (original-mechanics campaign; the corrected-A2C router re-run
is queued), the router produced large negative returns on two of three
seeds ($-19.0\%$, $-15.1\%$) and a negative mean Sharpe ($-0.060$),
below even the regime-agnostic baseline. Under PPO --- corrected-era,
with trust-region control (Table~\ref{tab:ppo}) --- the pattern
\emph{replicates}: seed 2 collapses ($-27.8\%$, Sharpe $-0.281$), and
the router's variance is $1.6$--$2.5\times$ that of either gated variant
on every metric. Both gated variants beat the router under both
algorithms. This is the one architecture-level result that survives
every control in the paper: \textbf{jointly learning the routing
function destabilises training relative to consuming a stable,
separately estimated signal --- independently of the policy optimiser}.

Under PPO the Soft-vs-Hard Sharpe gap is a statistical tie (0.774 vs
0.726) and Hard's seed instability is absent. In hindsight, PPO was
telling us early what the L2-matched control later confirmed: the PPO
arms carried the \emph{same} regularisation asymmetry (Hard at 0.5, Soft
at 0.01), yet tied anyway --- the clipped objective bounds per-update
policy movement and thereby suppresses sensitivity to the mis-set
coefficient that A2C's unconstrained updates amplify. Two lessons
follow. First, \textbf{the optimiser moved results far more than any
architecture did}: switching A2C$\rightarrow$PPO lifts every variant by
${\sim}{+}0.3$ Sharpe, an order of magnitude larger than the largest
matched-arm architecture gap (${\sim}0.04$--$0.06$). Second, a
methodological caution: \textbf{stability under a trust-region method
cannot be read as evidence that a configuration is sound} --- PPO masked
a regularisation problem that A2C exposed, and only the matched control
identified its true source.

\subsection{Walk-Forward Validation}
\label{sec:results-wf}

The walk-forward protocol delivers the study's most instructive
\emph{negative} result (Table~\ref{tab:wf}): with corrected mechanics,
the regime gate adds nothing. The no-gate baseline (aggregate Sharpe
1.270) statistically ties Hard (1.265) and edges Soft (1.105); no
RL-vs-RL comparison approaches significance under Wilcoxon over the 8
paired windows (all $p \geq 0.55$) or under Jobson--Korkie--Memmel on
the pooled ${\sim}1000$-day daily return series (e.g.\ Soft vs Baseline
$z{=}{-}0.60$, $p{=}0.55$). Equal weight remains the aggregate winner
(1.645, lowest drawdown) --- the only significant aggregate fact in the
protocol (vs.\ S\&P~500, $p{=}0.0078$).

A hyperparameter-parity check strengthens the null. The walk-forward
trained all arms at shared defaults (LR $10^{-4}$, L2 0.5); one might
object that Soft's tuned single-period configuration (LR
$3{\times}10^{-5}$, L2 0.01) would fare better. Re-running the soft arm
at exactly that configuration, same seeds and budget, made it
\emph{worse}: mean Sharpe 0.73 vs.\ 1.08 over the first seven windows,
now significantly below the no-gate baseline (Wilcoxon $p{=}0.016$),
including a $-53\%$ blow-up in the 2022 bear-onset window where the
looser regularisation permitted concentrated positions exactly when the
HMM's regime typing was most wrong (see below). The walk-forward null is
robust to hyperparameter choice --- if anything the shared defaults
flattered the gated arm --- and the check illustrates a transfer
failure worth naming: \textbf{hyperparameters tuned under one evaluation
protocol do not transfer to another} (a configuration tuned for
1000-epoch single-period training undertrains and under-regularises
300-epoch expanding windows).

Two window-level claims from the original (pre-correction) version of
this study die here, and we report them as cautionary results. First,
the COVID window: under the original mechanics, Soft appeared uniquely
crisis-robust (Sharpe 1.00 vs Hard 0.05 vs Baseline $-0.44$) ---
seemingly the textbook case for regime awareness. Under corrected
mechanics, \emph{every} variant weathers W1 (Baseline 1.11, Hard 1.12)
and Soft is the laggard (0.68). Second, Hard's anomalous H2-2022 spike
(2.83) normalises to 0.95. A transition-day decomposition ($\pm2$ days
around HMM state switches) shows all methods lose on transition days
(Sharpe $-0.9$ to $-2.0$, vs $+1.2$ to $+1.6$ on stable days), with
Hard nominally least-bad --- descriptive only, as between-method
transition differences are not significant.

\textbf{Synthesis with Section~\ref{sec:results-single}.} The two
protocols now agree: with mechanics corrected and regularisation
matched, no gated variant measurably beats the no-gate control on
either. An earlier draft of this study read the single-period soft
advantage as evidence that gating pays precisely when a policy must
generalise across regime shifts it cannot be retrained through; the
matched-regularisation control and the no-gate baseline dissolved that
reading. What survives is the weaker but well-supported observation
that \textbf{frequently retrained policies carry recent-regime
information in their weights} --- every RL arm, gated or not, posts
high absolute walk-forward Sharpe because six-monthly refitting keeps
it adapted --- so any residual value of an explicit gate must be sought
where retraining is infrequent relative to regime turnover, and our
single-period test bounds that value at below seed noise for this
system.

\subsection{Why RL at All? The Gated Equal-Weight Rule}
\label{sec:results-gatedew}

If the HMM signal were directly monetizable, a hand-crafted rule should
capture the value without RL. It does not (Table~\ref{tab:gatedew}): on
the single-period test, bear-gating \emph{cuts} equal-weight's Sharpe
from 0.41 to 0.16 (hard rule) or 0.19 (soft rule), while the soft-gated
RL agent's mean Sharpe (0.462) exceeds plain equal weight --- though the
latter comparison is seed-dependent (one of three seeds drives it) and
we claim it only at the mean level. On the walk-forward protocol the
rule is likewise (mildly) value-destroying. The rule's failure closes
the last escape route for the signal: it is not directly monetizable by
a transparent strategy, and Section~\ref{sec:results-single} shows the
learned agents extract no measurable value from it either --- the RL
arms' performance comes from what they learn about the assets, not from
the regime label.

\subsection{The Artifact Audit: Mechanics, then Regularisation}
\label{sec:results-mechanics}

Under the original stride-1 sliding-window updates, Soft RA-RL showed a
maximum-drawdown advantage over Hard that was the single most
significant result in the study (23.8\% vs 36.2\%, paired
$p{=}0.0025$, $n{=}9$). Under non-overlapping batches the ordering
\emph{reverses} under A2C (Hard $48.9\%$ vs Soft $57.2\%$, reversed in
all three seed-pairs) --- and independently reverses under PPO (36.4\%
vs 41.7\%), which never used the sliding buffer. The
risk-adjusted-return advantage survived the mechanics correction in
direction and effect size --- and then collapsed to a tie under the
matched-L2 control (Table~\ref{tab:single-a2c}). Each control caught
what the previous one could not.

The walk-forward protocol was hit even harder. Under the original
mechanics the no-gate baseline appeared broken (aggregate Sharpe 0.35),
Soft ranked best among RL variants (1.35), and Soft looked uniquely
crisis-robust in the COVID window --- three findings that together made
a clean pro-regime-gating story. Correcting the update scheme repaired
the \emph{baseline} most of all (0.35 $\rightarrow$ 1.27) and dissolved
all three claims (Section~\ref{sec:results-wf}).

The full ledger is worth stating plainly. Six publishable-looking
results --- (i) the significant drawdown advantage, (ii) the
walk-forward ranking, (iii) the COVID stress-test story, (iv) Hard's
anomalous H2-2022 spike, (v) soft-over-hard risk-adjusted dominance,
and (vi) Hard's seed instability --- were artifacts of, respectively,
one update-scheme choice (i--iv) and one asymmetrically tuned
regularisation coefficient (v--vi), both orthogonal to the
architectures nominally under study. The lesson is methodological:
\textbf{training-mechanics and tuning-symmetry choices can manufacture
an internally consistent, statistically significant, but false
architectural narrative --- and each layer of it is only caught by the
corresponding control.} We recommend mechanics ablations, matched
per-arm regularisation, and a no-gate control as standard robustness
passes for architectural claims in RL for finance, alongside seeds and
algorithm changes.

%% ===================================================================
\section{Discussion}
%% ===================================================================

\subsection{The 1/N Puzzle, Revisited}
Equal-weight achieves the highest aggregate walk-forward Sharpe of any
method, replicating DeMiguel et al.~\cite{demiguel2009}: five of eight
windows are bull or strong-recovery periods where full diversification
is near-optimal and active reallocation adds estimation noise. Our
claims are therefore scoped to \emph{active, RL-based} strategies. One
qualification: the 1/N dominance is protocol-dependent --- on the
2022--2024 single-period test, plain 1/N (Sharpe 0.410) is below the
soft-gated agent's mean (0.462), though seed-dependently. The
original-mechanics finding that Soft beat 1/N in the COVID window did
not survive the correction (Section~\ref{sec:results-wf}); in the
corrected walk-forward the RL variants beat 1/N in W1 (all ${\geq}0.68$
vs 0.33) but 1/N wins the aggregate.

\subsection{Interpretation: Why Doesn't the Gate Pay Here?}
\label{sec:interpretation}
The null is not mysterious; four measurable mechanisms account for it,
and each points at a condition a regime gate would need to be useful.
\textbf{(1)~The posterior is nearly always one-hot.} The HMM's
smoothed posterior exceeds 0.99 on 78--85\% of test days, so soft
blending degenerates to hard switching almost everywhere; the
mechanisms can only differ on a handful of transition days ---
precisely the days on which the detector is least reliable. (2)~\textbf{The
regime features are recoverable from the state.} The HMM consumes
20-day rolling volatility, momentum, mean return, and average pairwise
correlation; the agent's state contains the full 20-day return
covariance --- from which volatility and correlation are directly
computable --- plus momentum-type indicators, over the same window. The
gate therefore adds little information the trunk cannot synthesise
internally, which is exactly what the information-removal baseline
finds. (3)~\textbf{Rare-regime experts are starved.} Because each
head's gradient is scaled by its regime's posterior mass, and
train-period occupancy is $[0.025, 0.19, 0.28, 0.51]$ across the four
states, the bear head receives ${\sim}20\times$ less training signal
than the bull head --- specialist heads decay toward clones of the
majority policy. (4)~\textbf{The action space cannot spend regime
knowledge.} The agent must hold $\sum_i w_i = 1$ in equities at all
times (5\% max short per name); regime information of the kind an HMM
on bonds, commodities, and credit provides is asset-\emph{allocation}
information, and the classical channel through which regime knowledge
pays --- risk-off rotation~\cite{ang2002regimes} --- is not expressible.
In 2022, when equity--bond correlation inverted, every fully-invested
variant drew down 49--63\% regardless of architecture.

The router's cross-algorithm collapse and the gated-rule failure still
carry design content: regime \emph{detection}, if used, should be
external and stable rather than jointly learned, and no transparent
rule monetises the signal directly. But conditions (1)--(4) define what
a system would need before an architectural comparison of gates is even
meaningful: a calibrated detector that is uncertain when it should be,
regime features not already in the state, balanced training across
regimes, and an action space spanning the regimes' optimal portfolios.
We conjecture --- and leave as future work --- that multi-asset
universes with risk-off assets are where regime gating has its best
chance of measurable value.

\subsection{Limitations}
(1)~Single asset class and fixed 38-stock universe with survivorship
bias; frictionless execution beyond a linear turnover penalty. As
Section~\ref{sec:interpretation} argues, this setting also bounds how
much any regime signal \emph{could} pay --- our null is a statement
about this class of system, not about regime information in general.
(2)~Small seed counts: $n{=}3$ single-period ($n{=}2$ for the
baseline's third seed, pending compute), so effects below
${\sim}0.2$--$0.3$ Sharpe are undetectable there; the walk-forward
($n{=}8$ windows, JK--Memmel on ${\sim}1000$ daily returns) carries the
inferential weight. Wilcoxon at $n{=}8$ has a $p$-floor of 0.0078.
(3)~Test-period regime posteriors use forward--backward smoothing over
the full series (the hmmlearn default), a mild lookahead: measured
$L_1$ deviation from causal filtering is 0.08--0.13 with 4--8\% of MAP
labels flipped. The bias \emph{favours} the gated variants, so the
nulls hold a fortiori.
(4)~The evaluation window (2020--2024) contains exactly one
flight-to-quality crash and one rate-driven bear; a probe of fixed
defensive-rotation rules shows they help in the former (+0.30
window Sharpe, COVID) and destroy value in the latter ($-1.21$, 2022,
JK--Memmel $p{=}0.042$ against the rule) --- regime \emph{type}
matters. Relatedly, the HMM fitted through 2021 assigns the 2022 bear
${\sim}0\%$ bear-state probability (it presents as ``sideways-down''),
so the detector is confidently wrong in the test period's hardest
stretch.
(5)~Absolute Sharpe levels are modest by institutional standards; the
comparison is relative, under an identical reward.

%% ===================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%% ===================================================================

Given an external HMM regime signal, we compared hard switching, soft
posterior gating, end-to-end learned routing, and a no-gate control for
RL portfolio policies, under two optimisers, two evaluation protocols,
seed-paired statistics, a training-mechanics ablation, and matched
regularisation. Our central result is a carefully established null:
\textbf{once mechanics, regularisation, and hyperparameters are
controlled, no way of injecting the regime signal --- including not
injecting it at all --- measurably changes performance}, on either
protocol. What moved results, every time, was something else: the
update scheme (six dissolved claims, one at $p{=}0.0025$), a per-arm
regularisation coefficient, the optimiser (${+}0.3$ Sharpe from
A2C$\rightarrow$PPO, dwarfing all architecture gaps), and the
evaluation protocol. The findings that survive are the negative
space: jointly learned routing is unstable under both optimisers
(anchor any gate to an external signal), transparent rules on the same
signal destroy value, and frequently retrained policies carry regime
information in their weights without any gate. We further measure
\emph{why} this system's gate cannot pay --- near-one-hot posteriors,
$20\times$ expert-occupancy imbalance, state-redundant regime features,
and a fully-invested single-asset-class action space that cannot
express regime rotation --- conditions we propose as a checklist for
when a regime-gating comparison is meaningful at all. We offer the
full protocol (paired seeds, two algorithms, two protocols, mechanics
ablation, tuning-symmetry control, no-gate control, rule baseline) as
a template for architectural claims in RL for finance. Future work:
multi-asset universes where risk-off rotation makes regime knowledge
expressible~\cite{ang2002regimes}, bear-type-aware detection,
remediated expert architectures (deeper heads, occupancy-balanced
training), and off-policy algorithms (a SAC suite is implemented and
calibrated; its full evaluation is deferred).
